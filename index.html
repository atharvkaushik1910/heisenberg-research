01 // THE PROBLEM

Classical Computing Has Limits

Modern computing has transformed science, industry, and civilization itself. But some problems remain fundamentally beyond the reach of even the largest classical supercomputers.

Accurate molecular simulation, large-scale optimization, quantum chemistry, advanced materials, cryptography, and complex physical systems all scale exponentially on classical hardware.

Quantum computing offers a fundamentally new computational model — but building a useful quantum computer requires solving one of the hardest engineering problems ever attempted: Fault tolerance.

Error Correction Is the Bottleneck

Quantum systems are naturally fragile. Environmental noise, decoherence, thermal interactions, and operational imperfections introduce errors continuously during computation.

Traditional superconducting architectures require extremely large error-correction overheads, often demanding thousands of physical qubits to create a single reliable logical qubit. This scaling challenge is the defining bottleneck of the industry. Heisenberg Research is developing a fundamentally different approach.

02 // WHY CAT QUBITS

Hardware-Efficient Fault Tolerance

01

Hardware-Efficient Fault Tolerance

Heisenberg Research develops stabilized cat qubit architectures designed to dramatically reduce the overhead required for quantum error correction.

Instead of treating all quantum errors equally, cat qubits introduce a strong noise bias that exponentially suppresses bit-flip errors at the hardware layer.

This transforms quantum error correction from a two-dimensional problem into a far more hardware-efficient one-dimensional architecture.

02

Autonomous Stabilization

Our architecture encodes quantum information inside coherent states stabilized within superconducting microwave resonators using engineered two-photon dissipation mechanisms.

This autonomous stabilization continuously protects quantum information directly at the physical layer.

Reduced physical-to-logical overhead
More scalable logical qubits
Lower energy consumption
Simpler error-correction architectures
Faster routes toward useful computation
03

From 2D to 1D Architectures

Conventional superconducting systems rely heavily on surface-code architectures requiring large two-dimensional qubit grids.

Cat qubits allow error correction to operate in one dimension through repetition-code approaches while remaining compatible with advanced LDPC connectivity strategies.

This significantly reduces the hardware complexity required for scalable fault-tolerant systems.
